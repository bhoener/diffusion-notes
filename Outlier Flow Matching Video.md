
We have a dataset of images and want to approximate it using a probability distribution.

We can call the underlying probability distribution of our dataset $p_1(x_1)$. We generate new data by starting at a known distribution $p_0(x_0)$ we can sample from and using a neural network to turn the sample into a data-like sample.


We want a neural network that can learn the trajectory between $p_0(x_0)$ and $p_1(x_1)$. 

The simplest path between the noisy data $x_0$ and real data $x_1$  is via linear interpolation.

$$
x_t = tx_1 + (1-t)x_0
$$
We want to find how this path changes wrt $t$. 

$$
\frac{d}{dt}x_t = \frac{d}{dt}\left[tx_1 + (1-t)x_0\right]
= x_1 - x_0
$$We could have a model that takes in $x_t$ and predicts $x_1 - x_0$. 


### Bigger picture

All diffusion-related papers are trying to learn $p_1(x_1)$. This includes learning $p_t(x_t)$ in between. 

Score matching:

$$
\nabla_{x_t} \log p_t(x_t)
$$
DDPM:
$$
-\log p_t(x_t)
$$

Flow matching:
$$
\psi_t(x) \rightarrow p_t(x_t)
$$

### Derivation

We have some distribution $p_t(x_t)$ which is generated by "the flow" $x_t=\psi_t(x)$. We can obtain $x$ from the flow.

We can take a time derivative and introduce a function $u_t$

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\psi_t(x) = u_t(\psi_t(x))
$$
If we replace $\psi_t(x)$ with $x_t$, we have

$$
\frac{d}{dt}x_t = u_t(x_t)
$$
This means the way $x_t$ changes is determined by the vector field $u_t$. This field points in the direction from $x_t$ to data.

We do not know this vector field, so we try to approximate it with a neural network $v_t(x_t)$. 

We can do this by minimizing the objective

$$
\mathbb{E}_{x_t \sim p_t(x_t)}[||v_t(x_t) - u_t(x_t)||_2^2]
$$
However, we don't know $p_t(x_t)$ or $u_t(x_t)$. 

We can do some math tricks


$$
\mathbb{E}_{x_t \sim p_t(x_t)}[||v_t(x_t)||^2 - 2\cdot v_t(x_t) \cdot u_t(x_t) + ||u_t(x_t)||^2]
$$
$$
\mathbb{E}_{x_t \sim p_t(x_t)}[2 \cdot v_t(x_t) \cdot u_t(x_t)] = 2 \int v_t(x_t) \cdot u_t(x_t) \cdot p_t(x_t) dx_t
$$
With marginalization,

$$
u_t(x_t) = \int \underbrace{u_t(x_t|x_1)}_\text{single datapoint} \hspace{2mm} \underbrace{\frac{p_t(x_t|x_1)q(x_1)}{p_t(x_t)}}_\text{weight}dx_1
$$

Note that the right hand side is still equal to $u_t(x_t)$, meaning it is a vector field pointing towards data from our current position.

The $\frac{p_t(x_t|x_1)q(x_1)}{p_t(x_t)}$ represents the likelihood of the datapoint $x_1$ being at position $x_t$ at time $t$.
